\title{LangClaw: Endogenous Homeostatic Regulation for Mitigating Context
Collapse in Multi-Agent LLM Debate Systems}

\begin{abstract}
Multi-agent LLM systems tend to lose coherence as interaction extends---a
problem we term \emph{context collapse}. Existing frameworks address this
through static orchestration or periodic heartbeats, but both treat
participation frequency as a design constant. We ask whether it can instead
be an emergent property of each agent's internal regulatory state.
We present LangClaw, a framework grounded in a formal theory of homeostatic
agency in which each agent maintains an epistemic deficit
$\delta_i \in [\varepsilon,\infty)$ governed by a drive function
$D(\delta) = (\delta - \varepsilon)^2$; reward is defined as drive reduction,
implementing the equivalence theorem of Keramati and Gutkin (2014). A
multi-criteria stimulus evaluator acts as sensor; an online Q-learner
with $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration selects actions via TD(0). This
represents a structural departure from the prevailing heartbeat paradigm:
rather than firing on a fixed schedule, agents activate when internal
pressure warrants it and learn which actions most effectively reduce drive.
We evaluate LangClaw against a LangGraph-style LLM router under equal
heartbeat budgets in a zero-sum political debate with ten GPT-5-nano agents
organized as two VSM-inspired factions, measuring discourse structure through
AAF metrics, graph-structural peer reference, and the quality proxy
$\Delta\varphi^*$.

\keywords{multi-agent systems \and large language models \and homeostasis \and
reinforcement learning \and context collapse \and event-driven architecture}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-agent LLM systems are moving toward sustained, deliberative
interaction---domains such as policy analysis, scientific peer review, and
institutional decision support where coherence over time is socially
consequential~\parencite{wang2024survey}. Two developments make this tractable
now: structured output enforcement enables closed regulatory loops over LLM
agents (unreliable before 2024), and scaling laws for inference-time
compute~\parencite{snell2024scaling} raise a distributional question: given a
fixed budget, how should computation be allocated across agents?

Yet as interaction extends, the collective process tends to degrade. Agents
may lose track of prior claims, repeat points, or produce structurally
disconnected monologues~\parencite{park2023generative}---a class of failures
we term \emph{context collapse}. The problem has a sociological precedent:
Michels' iron law of oligarchy~\parencite{michels1911political} established
that centralized coordination in deliberative organizations systematically
concentrates influence among contextually salient contributors. The mechanism
is informational, not intentional, and may apply equally to AI routers.

The dominant response has been external control---fixed turn schedules,
centralized routers, heartbeat daemons~\parencite{openclaw2026heartbeat}.
These approaches share a structural assumption: the activation decision
remains exogenous. The heartbeat fires at a fixed interval regardless of
discourse intensity; the router selects agents regardless of their internal
readiness. External orchestration may thus address the symptom without
explaining why coherence degrades.

A well-established phenomenon in biological systems offers a different
perspective: homeostasis. In living organisms, regulation emerges from
internal variables that accumulate, trigger action, and return to equilibrium
--- without an external controller. On its own, this observation belongs to
physiology. \emph{What if multi-agent coherence could be regulated
endogenously rather than imposed externally?} Homeostatic reinforcement
learning (HRRL)~\parencite{keramati2014homeostatic} has formalized this
principle, yet it has not been applied to LLM-based multi-agent
orchestration. The question is not only technical: it contrasts two paradigms
of coordination. If coherence depends on internal regulation rather than
external scheduling, many current strategies may be addressing the symptom
rather than the cause.

To explore this, we propose LangClaw, in which each agent carries an
\emph{epistemic deficit} that grows when idle and shrinks after a
structurally useful contribution. A sigmoid gate maps deficit to action
probability; an online Q-learner selects actions and learns from drive
reduction. To the best of our knowledge, this paradigm has not been evaluated
as an orchestration principle for LLM-based debate agents.

More specifically, this paper makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A theory of homeostatic agency.} The first formalization
          of multi-agent autonomy grounded in systemic materialism, allostatic
          regulation, and eudaimonic motivation research. Rather than treating
          autonomy and drive as informal intuitions, we derive them as
          ontological postulates with testable predictions---making the design
          decisions of the framework scientifically explicit and falsifiable.
    \item \textbf{An endogenous activation paradigm.} A homeostatic loop in
          which participation frequency is an emergent property of the
          agent's internal regulatory state, not a design constant. This
          replaces the heartbeat/scheduler paradigm---where activation cadence
          is externally imposed---with deficit-driven, stimulus-responsive
          Q-learning in which agents activate when internal pressure warrants
          it and learn which actions most effectively reduce drive.
    \item \textbf{Empirical evidence of the Michels dynamic in AI
          orchestration.} An experimental demonstration that neutral external
          routers systematically concentrate turns on the contextually dominant
          faction---replicating at the algorithmic level the informational
          concentration Michels documented in deliberative organizations---and
          that endogenous homeostatic regulation counters this tendency without
          explicit fairness constraints.
\end{enumerate}

For the sake of clarity: Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work;
Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the theory, agent architecture, and
orchestration design; Section~\ref{sec:setup} describes the experimental
protocol; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports outcomes; and
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets findings and limitations.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:related}

The literature motivating this work spans four connected problems: how
multi-agent LLM systems are orchestrated, how autonomous regulation is
formalized in reinforcement learning, how persistent agents are implemented in
practice, and how argumentative interaction is evaluated structurally.

The first strand concerns orchestration in LLM-based multi-agent systems.
Frameworks such as AutoGen~\parencite{wu2023autogen},
CAMEL~\parencite{li2023camel}, and MetaGPT~\parencite{hong2023metagpt}
demonstrate that role-specialized LLM collectives can coordinate on complex
tasks, but they typically rely on predefined turn structures, centralized
controllers, or fixed communication protocols. This makes them excellent
examples of \emph{externally organized} cooperation, but less suitable for
studying whether activation can emerge from the internal state of each agent.
This distinction matters because orchestration choice is not neutral:
\textcite{huang2024masresilience} show that organizational form affects
resilience under stress, with linear-chain structures degrading substantially
faster than more distributed ones.

The second strand concerns endogenous regulation. Homeostatic reinforcement
learning (HRRL)~\parencite{keramati2014homeostatic} provides a formal mechanism
for linking deviation from a setpoint to reward via drive reduction, while
continuous variants extend the same logic to richer dynamical
settings~\parencite{laurencon2024ctcs}. Related work on desire-driven
LLM agents~\parencite{wang2025d2a} also treats internal variables as relevant to
behavior, but without closing the loop through an explicit structural quality
signal and drive-reduction reward. The key gap is therefore not whether LLM
agents can be given internal variables, but whether those variables can govern
activation and learning in a mathematically explicit regulatory loop.

The third strand concerns persistent agent infrastructure. The heartbeat
architecture~\parencite{openclaw2026heartbeat} turns a reactive agent into a
temporally persistent one by periodically waking it to inspect context, while
LangGraph provides durable state, memory, and workflow control. These advances
are crucial for long-horizon agentic systems, but they still leave the
activation locus outside the agent: the heartbeat is fired externally and the
router decides externally. LangClaw builds directly on this infrastructure
line, but moves the decision to activate from the orchestration shell into the
agent's own regulatory mechanism.

The fourth strand concerns argumentative structure. Our discourse model is
informed by computational argumentation, especially Defeasible Logic
Programming (DeLP)~\parencite{simari1992mathematical,chesnevar2000logically,garcia2004defeasible}---a
line of research with foundational roots in Argentine AI, where Simari,
Chesne\-var, and García developed the core formal apparatus for defeasible
inference and argumentation-based reasoning. Claims are defeasible and may
be attacked by rebuttal or undercut. At
the same time, our experimental setting inherits the adversarial, socially
embedded spirit of frameworks such as Sotopia~\parencite{zhou2024sotopia},
although our focus is not social realism per se but the structural evolution of
multi-agent argument graphs. This is why the paper emphasizes defeat cycles,
acceptance dynamics, and quality of structural engagement rather than only task
completion or persona consistency.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{A Theory of Homeostatic Agency}
\label{sec:theory}

We model the multi-agent discourse system $\Sigma$ as a concrete system
$\langle \mathcal{C}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{M}
\rangle$~\parencite{bunge1977treatise}, where
$\mathcal{C} = \{a_1,\ldots,a_N\}$ are agents with scalar internal state
$\delta_i \in [\varepsilon, \infty)$;
$\mathcal{E}$ is the discourse environment (topic, LLM interface, observers);
$\mathcal{S}_{\text{int}} \subseteq \mathcal{C} \times \mathcal{C}$ are
argumentative relations and $\mathcal{S}_{\text{ext}}$ are agent--environment
relations; and $\mathcal{M}$ comprises the processes---deficit accumulation,
stimulus evaluation, action selection, satiation---that produce coordination.
The system state is $s(\Sigma,t) = (\delta_1(t),\ldots,\delta_N(t), G(t))$
where $G(t) = (V(t), E(t))$ is the discourse graph.
A property $P$ is \emph{emergent} iff
$P(\Sigma) \wedge \forall\, a_i: \neg P(a_i)$~\parencite{bunge1977treatise}.
Discourse coherence and \emph{participation frequency} are both emergent:
neither is a property of any single agent.

\subsubsection{Postulates.}

\noindent\textsc{P1} (Autonomy,~\parencite{ashby1956introduction,beer1972brain}).
$a_i$ is \emph{autonomous} iff $\pi_i = \pi_i(\delta_i(t))$; external
schedulers set $\partial\pi_i/\partial\delta_i = 0$, collapsing
requisite variety to zero.

\noindent\textsc{P2} (Drive,~\parencite{sterling1988allostasis,friston2010free}).
Each $a_i$ has a drive $D: S_L \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ with
$D(\varepsilon)=0$ and $D'(\delta_i)>0$; activation probability
$p_i(t)=\sigma(D(\delta_i(t)))$ increases monotonically in drive.

\noindent\textsc{P3} (Quality-gating,~\parencite{ryan2001happiness,huta2014eudaimonia}).
Drive reduction requires purpose-congruent action: $g(\mathcal{S}(t),
\mathcal{S}(t{+}1))=0 \Rightarrow \Delta\delta_i=0$. An isolated
monologue leaves drive unrelieved; only structural engagement provides
relief.

\noindent\textsc{P4} (Measurability,~\parencite{tononi2016integrated}).
$\Delta_s = g(\mathcal{S}(t), \mathcal{S}(t{+}1))$ is computable from
the discourse graph; instantiated as Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi}. Combined with
P3: $\Delta\delta_i = -\alpha\Delta_s$ when $\Delta_s>0$, else $0$.

% --- Phase 4: Logical closure ---

\subsubsection{Derived predictions.}

Two testable consequences follow from P1--P4. From P1--P2: symmetric basal
drive parameters produce symmetric activation tendency across all agents;
an external router has no equivalent symmetry guarantee, since it conditions
activation on contextual salience rather than internal state. From P2--P4:
if $\Delta_s > 0$ on average and drive is monotonically increasing, the
deficit is bounded and does not diverge, implying sustained participation
and preventing unilateral silence---a necessary condition for context
collapse. These predictions are operationalized in Section~\ref{sec:setup}
and tested in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{The Epistemic Drive}

A scalar epistemic deficit instantiates P2: interpretable (how much the
agent ``needs'' to contribute), quality-coupled via satiation (P3), and
grounded in HRRL~\parencite{keramati2014homeostatic}---where drive
minimization is equivalent to reward maximization.

Each agent $i$ maintains epistemic deficit $\delta_i \in [\varepsilon, \infty)$
with resting baseline $\varepsilon = 0.1$. Five coupled mechanisms govern it.

\textbf{Decay.} At each heartbeat $t$:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_i^{(t+1)} = \delta_i^{(t)} + \lambda
    \label{eq:decay}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda = 0.05$, modeling the basal pressure to contribute.

\textbf{Sigmoid activation.} The probability of acting at heartbeat $t$:
\begin{equation}
    p_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k(\delta_i^{(t)} - \theta)}}
    \label{eq:sigmoid}
\end{equation}
with $k = 10$, $\theta = 0.7$. Below $\theta$ the agent rests; above it,
the agent is compelled to participate.

\textbf{Stimulation.} A discourse event with relevance $r$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:stimulus})
pushes the deficit up:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_i^{(t+)} = \delta_i^{(t)} + \gamma \cdot r
    \label{eq:stimulate}
\end{equation}
with $\gamma = 0.1$. The deficit now integrates basal decay and discourse-driven
stimuli, making it a sensor of the agent's epistemic state relative to the
evolving debate.

\textbf{Satiation.} After producing an argument with quality $\Delta\varphi^*$:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_i^{\text{new}} = \max\!\bigl(\varepsilon,\;
        \delta_i - \alpha \cdot \Delta\varphi^*\bigr)
    \label{eq:satiation}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is selected by the calibration procedure (Section~\ref{sec:setup}).
If $\Delta\varphi^* = 0$, the deficit does not decrease (P3): speaking without
engaging the discourse structure provides no regulatory relief.

\textbf{Drive function.} Following~\textcite{keramati2014homeostatic}:
\begin{equation}
    D(\delta_i) = (\delta_i - \varepsilon)^m, \quad m = 2
    \label{eq:drive}
\end{equation}
The quadratic form penalizes deviations from setpoint superlinearly, creating
stronger urgency to act as deficit grows.

\textbf{Homeostatic reward.} Reward is not an external signal but drive
reduction~\parencite{keramati2014homeostatic}:
\begin{equation}
    r_t = D(\delta_t) - D(\delta_{t+1})
    \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
By the equivalence theorem, a policy maximizing expected discounted reward
simultaneously minimizes expected future drive.

\textbf{Online Q-learning.} State features
$\phi(s) = [\delta,\; \delta^2,\; \rho_G,\; n_{\text{stim}}]$~\label{eq:features}
(graph density $\rho_G$, buffered stimuli $n_{\text{stim}}$); linear
Q-function $Q(s,a) = \mathbf{w}_a^\top \phi(s)$; TD(0) update:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{w}_a \leftarrow \mathbf{w}_a + \eta \bigl[r + \gamma \max_{a'}
    Q(s', a') - Q(s, a)\bigr] \phi(s)
    \label{eq:td0}
\end{equation}
Weights are zero-initialized; action selection is $\varepsilon$-greedy
($\varepsilon=0.1$). Convergence within the horizon is not assumed; weights
are interpreted as adaptive control parameters, not a converged policy.

\subsection{Argument Graph and $\Phi^*$ Proxy}

Surface metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, BERTScore) capture fluency, not structural
integration. Following P4, we construct a computable proxy $\Delta\varphi^*$
that operationalizes structural engagement in the discourse graph
$G=(V,E)$, where nodes are defeasible arguments~\parencite{garcia2004defeasible}
and edges are attack relations. When agent $i$ adds node $v$ attacking target $u$:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta\varphi^* = \tfrac{1}{3} C_B(u) + \tfrac{1}{3}
        \mathbb{1}_{\text{cycle}}(v,u) + \tfrac{1}{3} D(u)
    \label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}

where $C_B(u)$ is betweenness centrality (rewards engaging structurally
central claims), $\mathbb{1}_{\text{cycle}}$ indicates a new dialectical
cycle (A attacks B, B attacks C, C attacks A), and $D(u)$ is agent diversity
at the target (rewards entering contested territory). Equal weights reflect a
maximum-entropy prior. Isolated nodes get $\Delta\varphi^* = 0$. We use
$\Delta\varphi^*$ strictly as an operational structural measure, not as a
claim about information integration in the IIT sense.

\subsection{Action Space and Stimulus Evaluation}

Each agent selects from five action types: \textbf{DEBATE} (produce an
argument, either stimulus-driven or proactive), \textbf{SEARCH} (acquire a
domain fact), \textbf{READ} (synthesize and consolidate recent graph state),
\textbf{MESSAGE} (send a directed communicative act to a specific allied
agent), or \textbf{PASS} (rest). This action set is identical in HRRL and
LangGraph; the difference between conditions lies in activation, not in local
capability.

Two subsystems govern action selection, each with a distinct role.

The \emph{StimulusEvaluator} acts as a \textbf{sensor}. When a
\texttt{NewArgumentEvent} arrives, it computes relevance across five
weighted criteria:

\begin{equation}
    U(\text{stimulus}) = w_F \cdot F + w_C \cdot C_B(u) + w_M \cdot M
        + w_N \cdot N + w_P \cdot P,
    \label{eq:stimulus}
\end{equation}
with $w_F + w_C + w_M + w_N + w_P = 1$.

\noindent where $F$ is faction relevance, $C_B(u)$ is betweenness
centrality of the attacked node, $M$ is strategic memory relevance, $N$ is
novelty (reduced if an ally already responded), and $P$ is unanswered
pressure. This relevance score feeds the \texttt{stimulate()} mechanism
(Eq.~\ref{eq:stimulate}), modulating the agent's deficit in response to
discourse events. The stimulus weights are not fixed ad hoc. We begin from an
equal-importance prior and then calibrate alternative weight profiles by
micro-simulation before the main benchmark (Section~\ref{sec:setup}).

The \emph{Q-learner} acts as the \textbf{decision maker}. After the
sigmoid gate passes (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigmoid}), the Q-learner selects an
action via $\varepsilon$-greedy policy (Sutton \& Barto, 2018, \S2.3):
with probability $\varepsilon = 0.1$ a uniformly random action is chosen
(exploration); otherwise $\arg\max_a Q(s,a)$ is taken (exploitation).
After execution, the homeostatic reward (Eq.~\ref{eq:reward}) provides
the TD update signal (Eq.~\ref{eq:td0}). Over successive heartbeats,
the Q-learner adapts its action preferences based on which actions most
effectively reduce drive.


\subsection{Three-Layer Memory}

Each agent maintains episodic memory (past experiences, semantic search),
semantic memory (facts from SEARCH actions), and working memory (sliding
window of recent arguments), all backed by LangGraph's
\texttt{InMemoryStore}. Both conditions share identical memory
infrastructure, isolating the orchestration mechanism as the sole
experimental variable.

\subsection{Event-Driven Architecture}

Each agent is an autonomous event-processing entity. Per heartbeat: buffered
\texttt{NewArgumentEvent}s are drained and $\delta_i$ incremented by decay;
the stimulus evaluator scores each event (Eq.~\ref{eq:stimulus}); the
sigmoid gate (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigmoid}) determines whether the top-valued
action executes; if it passes, the LLM produces the action output;
satiation and TD update close the loop (Eqs.~\ref{eq:satiation},
\ref{eq:td0}). Internal deliberation follows a
THINK--PLAN--EXECUTE--OBSERVE cycle. Figure~\ref{fig:agent_architecture}

\subsection{Orchestration Modes}

We compare two modes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HRRL} (endogenous): Each agent runs as an async coroutine
          that buffers incoming \texttt{NewArgumentEvent}s between heartbeats,
          stimulates its deficit proportionally to event relevance
          (Eq.~\ref{eq:stimulus}), gates execution through the
          deficit-sigmoid (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigmoid}), and selects actions via
          online Q-learning (Eq.~\ref{eq:td0}) with reward defined as
          drive reduction (Eq.~\ref{eq:reward}). No external scheduler
          decides who speaks.
    \item \textbf{LangGraph} (exogenous baseline): A neutral LLM router reads
          the current discourse state and selects which agent will enter the
          same cognitive loop on that heartbeat. The selected agent then
          executes the same local capabilities as in HRRL, including debate,
          search, read, pass, and directed messaging. The routing decision is
          made externally; the local action decision remains open-ended within
          the selected agent.
\end{itemize}

Both modes share the same memory, prompts, action space, cognitive phases,
and model backend. The sole difference is \emph{where} the activation
decision is made: endogenously (deficit, stimulus, Q-learning) or
exogenously (router LLM call, one additional call per heartbeat). Both run
for the same heartbeat horizon.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Evaluating multi-agent debate requires metrics that capture structural
properties of the argument graph, not just surface-level fluency or
embedding similarity. We use four established metrics and one internal proxy:

\textbf{AAF metrics (Dung, 1995).} Dung's semantics are the canonical
evaluation layer for computational argumentation; we use grounded semantics
(the most skeptical, uniquely determined extension) appropriate for
zero-sum debate. We compute two metrics:
(1)~the \emph{defeat cycle count} $\lvert\text{SCC}_{>1}\rvert$---the number
of strongly-connected components with more than one node, indicating mutual
refutation cycles, a hallmark of genuine dialectical engagement; and
(2)~the \emph{acceptance ratio} $\alpha = \lvert G \rvert / \lvert A \rvert$,
where $G$ is the grounded extension (computed via fixed-point iteration) and
$A$ is the set of all arguments. $\alpha = 1$ means all claims are
epistemically undefeated; $\alpha = 0$ means all claims are contested.

\textbf{Peer-Reference Rate, graph variant (PRR$_G$).} We use the
graph-structural variant of the Peer-Reference Rate
metric~\parencite{marandi2026prr}: the fraction of DEBATE turns in which the
agent's argument targets an existing node ($\text{target}(a) \in V$). We
chose the graph-structural variant because agents argue against
\emph{positions}, not named peers; the text-based
PRR~\parencite{marandi2026prr} yields zero in this setting and would not
discriminate between modes.

\textbf{$\Delta\varphi^*$ quality proxy} (Eq.~\ref{eq:phi}): our
network-theoretic measure of argumentative integration per intervention.

\textbf{Initiative Ratio (IR).} IR serves as a manipulation check, not an
outcome metric, confirming that the two modes are structurally distinct in
how activation decisions are made:
$\text{IR} = |\{t : \text{trigger}(t) = \text{HOMEOSTATIC}\}| / |\{t : \text{action}(t) \neq \text{PASS}\}|$.
IR $\approx 1.0$ for HRRL (all active turns self-initiated) and IR $= 0.0$
for LangGraph (all active turns externally routed).

Taken together, these metrics provide a lightweight observability layer for
multi-agent discourse: all are computable in real time from the graph
structure, without human annotation or model-based scoring. This property is
increasingly valued as agentic systems scale and the need for automated
oversight grows~\parencite{wang2024survey}.

\subsubsection{Central Hypothesis.}

The benchmark tests one central hypothesis: \emph{under equal heartbeat
budgets and identical local agent capabilities, endogenous homeostatic
regulation sustains autonomy and argumentative coherence over time more
robustly than exogenous routing}. Operationally, this means that HRRL should
show weaker temporal degradation in discourse quality and contestation than the
LangGraph baseline, while preserving genuine distributed agency rather than
collapsing activity into a few contextually salient actors.

\noindent Parameters with direct theoretical grounding ($\theta$, $\lambda$,
$k$, $\varepsilon$, $m$, $\eta$, $\gamma_{RL}$) were fixed from the formal
model or from standard RL practice. Parameters with no principled unique value
in advance --- specifically the stimulus-weight profile and the satiation rate
$\alpha$ --- were selected by a pre-benchmark calibration stage rather than by
arbitrary choice.

\subsection{Calibration Procedure}

Before the full benchmark, we run a micro-simulation ablation over the two
hyperparameter families most likely to bias agent behavior if fixed by fiat:
(1)~the stimulus-evaluator weights and (2)~the debate satiation coefficient
$\alpha$. The calibration grid consists of five pre-registered weight profiles
(equal prior; faction-dominant; centrality-dominant; novelty-dominant;
pressure-dominant) crossed with three $\alpha$ values, for a total of
15 HRRL-only micro-runs. Each micro-run uses 10 heartbeats and one fixed seed.
Configurations are ranked by mean $\Delta\varphi^*$, with debate consistency as
a tie-breaker. The best configuration is then frozen and used for the
multi-seed benchmark. This procedure does not tune against the LangGraph
baseline; it only prevents the HRRL implementation from embedding arbitrary
weight choices.

Agent outputs are structured as Pydantic-enforced JSON objects with fields
\texttt{action}, \texttt{claim}, \texttt{target\_node\_id},
\texttt{attack\_type}, \texttt{send\_to}, \texttt{message\_content},
\texttt{message\_type}. Failed parses trigger a deficit penalty of $+0.1$
after two retry attempts. The benchmark script runs both modes for each seed sequentially. A seed
factory derives deterministic prime seeds for each agent's RNG and LLM call
via SHA-256 hashing, ensuring reproducibility. We run five seeds
($s \in \{7, 17, 42, 123, 256\}$) and report mean $\pm$ standard deviation.
Statistical tests use one-sided Welch's $t$-test with Bonferroni correction.

\subsection{Structural Validity}

Before presenting outcome metrics, we verify that the two modes differ
structurally in the expected way. The Initiative Ratio (IR) is 1.0 for HRRL
and 0.0 for LangGraph across all five seeds, confirming that all active
turns under HRRL are self-initiated (trigger = HOMEOSTATIC) and all active
turns under LangGraph are externally chosen (trigger = ROUTER). This
manipulation check passes: the two modes are structurally distinct.

Table~\ref{tab:results} presents the aggregate results. With both modes
producing the same number of debates by design (74.0 on average), five
patterns stand out.

\textbf{H confirmed: balanced participation as emergent property.} HRRL
produces a mean per-seed per-agent standard deviation $\bar{\sigma}_s =
1.53 \pm 0.58$, compared to $6.80 \pm 1.14$ for LangGraph ($t = -8.1$,
$df = 5.9$, $p < 0.001$, $d = -5.1$). The effect is large and consistent
across all five seeds. HRRL distributes debates nearly uniformly
($\sim$18.4 per agent), whereas LangGraph's router exhibits systematic
bias toward opposition agents (OPP: $\sim$25 vs.\ GOV: $\sim$12). This
$2.1\times$ imbalance reflects the router's tendency to favor the
attacking faction --- an instance of the Michels dynamic, where the
coordinator's contextual-relevance heuristic amplifies the informational
advantage of the more salient faction.

\textbf{Comparable argument quality.} HRRL achieves $\Delta\varphi^* =
0.149 \pm 0.010$, comparable to LangGraph's $0.157 \pm 0.011$. LangGraph
achieves marginally higher per-intervention quality; this difference is
not statistically significant at $\alpha = 0.05$ but is directionally
consistent across seeds ($d = -0.84$). The data suggests that endogenous
regulation achieves near-equivalent quality to centralized routing without
the overhead of an external selection function.

\textbf{More contested discourse under HRRL.} HRRL produces a
significantly lower AAF acceptance ratio ($\alpha = 0.566 \pm 0.021$)
than LangGraph ($\alpha = 0.711 \pm 0.043$). This means more arguments
are contested under HRRL. The pattern is consistent with balanced
participation: when all agents contribute equally, more claims face
counter-arguments. Under LangGraph, government agents are underrepresented,
so more opposition claims go uncontested, inflating $\alpha$. A lower
acceptance ratio signals a more thoroughly contested discourse, not
inferior quality.

\textbf{Stimulus-driven behavior.} 86\% of HRRL debates (63.6/74.0) are
triggered by specific discourse events, consistent with genuine
stimulus-response behavior. The average drive-reduction reward is
$r = 0.154 \pm 0.015$, confirming DEBATE actions reduce epistemic drive
as predicted. Q-weight learning converges in direction: DEBATE\_STIMULUS
$= 0.247 \pm 0.076 >$ DEBATE\_PROACTIVE $= 0.186 \pm 0.044 >$ SEARCH $>$
READ, stable across all five seeds---consistent with the agent learning to
prioritize stimulus-responsive engagement over proactive exploration.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Results}

The results paint a nuanced picture. HRRL achieves equivalent argument
quality to LangGraph while producing structurally different---and in some
respects superior---participation dynamics. We address each finding in turn.

\textbf{Comparable quality with genuine learning.} HRRL achieves
$\Delta\varphi^* = 0.149 \pm 0.010$, comparable to LangGraph's
$0.157 \pm 0.011$ ($d = -0.84$). The Q-learner adapts action selection
online: by the end of the simulation, DEBATE\_STIMULUS consistently
receives the highest Q-value ($0.247 \pm 0.076$), confirming that the
agent learns to prioritize stimulus-driven engagement over proactive
exploration. The average homeostatic reward per active heartbeat
($r = 0.154$) is positive, confirming that DEBATE actions consistently
reduce drive, as predicted by the equivalence theorem. Convergence within
50 heartbeats is limited; the learned weights should be interpreted as an
adaptive prior.

\textbf{Balanced participation as an emergent property (H confirmed).}
HRRL produces mean per-seed $\bar{\sigma}_s = 1.53$, compared to $6.80$
for LangGraph ($p < 0.001$, $d = -5.1$). HRRL distributes debates nearly
uniformly across agents, whereas LangGraph concentrates activity on
opposition agents (OPP receiving $2.3\times$ more turns than GOV). This
imbalance is consistent with Michels' iron law applied to AI orchestration:
the external router, acting as neutral coordinator, selects agents whose
contributions appear most contextually relevant---and in a political debate,
the attacking faction's arguments consistently surface more salient context.
This is not a flaw in the router's design; it is the structural consequence
of centralized selection in an asymmetric discourse. Endogenous regulation
counters this tendency through symmetric homeostatic pressure: all agents
accumulate deficit at the same basal rate, independent of faction, so no
single faction accrues structural informational advantage in the activation
decision. Balanced participation is not engineered---it is an emergent
consequence of the symmetry of the regulatory mechanism.

\textbf{No defeat cycles in either mode.} The absence of AAF defeat cycles
in both modes reflects a structural feature of the debate rather than a
limitation of the orchestration mechanism. In a two-faction political debate,
the discourse follows an asymmetric attack pattern (opposition attacks
government claims; government defends). Symmetric refutation cycles would
require agents to explicitly counter-attack claims that already attacked
them, which the current prompting strategy does not encourage.

\textbf{Homeostatic oscillation confirmed.} HRRL deficits oscillate around
$\theta = 0.7$ (final means: 0.50--0.66), exhibiting the predicted
three-phase behavior. LangGraph deficits grow monotonically (final means:
2.1--3.6), confirming the absence of self-regulation.

From a broader perspective, the contrast is not simply between two
implementation choices but between two theories of coordination. One holds
that coordination is best imposed by an external controller with full
information. The other holds that coordination can emerge from local
regulation. The present results suggest that endogenous regulation achieves
equivalent quality to centralized routing while producing emergent properties
---balanced participation, homeostatic oscillation, stimulus-responsive
activation---that the centralized approach cannot produce by design.

A reasonable objection is that the deficit mechanism reduces to a dynamic
priority queue with self-adjusting weights. The Q-learner makes the
distinction sharper: a priority queue cannot learn; the deficit-driven
agent does. The Q-weights adapt online based on which actions most
effectively reduce drive, producing behavior that changes over the
course of the simulation. Priority queues rank; Q-learners adapt.

A related objection is that the mechanism resembles contextual bandits.
The distinction is structural: unlike bandits, the agent's state (deficit)
evolves endogenously through the drive function (Eq.~\ref{eq:drive}), and
the reward depends on state transitions (Eq.~\ref{eq:reward}), not just
action outcomes. The temporal discount factor $\gamma$ and the TD update
make this a sequential decision problem, not a stateless one.

A candid observation: we describe HRRL as ``endogenous'' regulation, but
the regulatory mechanism was designed exogenously by the researchers. The
deficit, threshold, and satiation parameters are designer choices---a
``mapmaker,'' in the terminology of~\textcite{goo2025abstraction}. What is
endogenous is the \emph{dynamics}: once the mechanism is in place, the
oscillatory equilibrium emerges from agent-environment interaction without
further external intervention. This distinction keeps the claim honest.

This structural departure can be stated precisely. Both the heartbeat
paradigm~\parencite{openclaw2026heartbeat} and LangGraph-style routing
externalize the activation decision: the agent does not control its own
pulse. LangClaw internalizes it. The heartbeat signal still fires
uniformly, but the agent's \emph{propensity to act} is governed by its
deficit. The data suggest that participation frequency becomes an emergent
variable rather than a design constant.

A complementary interpretation frames HRRL as an adaptive inference-time
compute allocator. Recent scaling laws for test-time
compute~\parencite{snell2024scaling} have established that performance
improves predictably when more tokens are invested at inference. In a
multi-agent setting, this raises a distributional question: given a fixed
compute budget, how should LLM calls be allocated across agents and time?
Centralized routing answers via a global selection function (one additional
LLM call per heartbeat). HRRL answers adaptively: agents consume compute only
when their stimulus-evaluated internal state warrants it, with no routing
overhead. Our results show that HRRL achieves equivalent quality to
centralized routing while distributing compute more uniformly across agents.
Under tighter compute budgets or larger agent populations, this
self-balancing property may become a decisive advantage.

\subsection{Limitations}

We identify eight limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Statistical power}: A post-hoc power analysis indicates
          $n \geq 12$ seeds would be needed to detect $d = 0.5$ at 80\%
          power; our $n=5$ is exploratory. Results should be interpreted as
          preliminary evidence, not confirmatory findings.
    \item \textbf{Q-learning convergence}: With $\eta = 0.01$, the Q-learner
          does not converge within the simulation horizon. The learned weights
          should be interpreted as bounded-run adaptive control parameters,
          not as a converged policy.
    \item \textbf{Defeat cycle sensitivity}: Defeat cycles are treated here as
          empirical outcomes, not assumptions. Whether they emerge depends on
          how agents exploit opportunities for reciprocal attack; this remains
          a substantive target for further study.
    \item \textbf{PRR text inapplicability}: The text-based PRR metric
          yields zero for both modes because agents argue against positions
          rather than naming peers by ID. The graph-structural variant
          (PRR$_G$) was reported instead.
    \item \textbf{$\Phi^*$ proxy design}: The proxy uses equal weights as a
          principled prior, but remains a hand-crafted structural measure.
          Future work could compare it against learned or human-validated
          quality functions.
    \item \textbf{LLM dependence}: Results are produced with GPT-5-nano and
          will vary with different models and prompting regimes.
    \item \textbf{Scale}: Although the present design scales beyond the earlier
          four-agent prototype, longer horizons and larger populations still
          remain to be tested.
    \item \textbf{Safety and externalities}: Self-regulating agents operate
          without external oversight during execution. The deficit mechanism
          could be gamed: agents that produce structurally well-placed but
          semantically vacuous arguments would still receive satiation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Further Research}

Several directions follow from this work. First, prompting strategies that
encourage symmetric counter-attacks would make defeat cycles testable.
Second, hyperparameter sensitivity studies for $\theta$, $\alpha$,
$\lambda$, and $\gamma$ would characterize robustness. Third, scaling to
larger agent populations and longer horizons would test whether
balanced participation holds at scale. Fourth, replacing the heuristic
$\Phi^*$ proxy with a learned quality function would improve the fidelity of
the homeostatic signal. Fifth, scaling the Q-learner across episodes
(transfer of learned weights across debate topics) and learning the drive
exponent $m$ online are natural extensions. Finally, evaluating HRRL under
constrained inference-time compute budgets, where the adaptive allocation of
LLM calls may become a decisive advantage over uniform scheduling, is a
particularly promising direction.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper examined a concrete question: whether context collapse in
multi-agent LLM debate is better approached through external scheduling or
through endogenous regulation. We introduced LangClaw as an experimental
framework for studying that question under controlled conditions, comparing a
homeostatic mechanism against a LangGraph-style exogenous router under equal
heartbeat budgets, while holding local agent capabilities constant through a
shared cognitive loop, memory system, action space, and VSM role structure.

The relevance of this question extends beyond the present prototype. If
coherence in multi-agent interaction depends mainly on external control, then
future systems will likely continue to evolve toward more sophisticated
routing and supervision layers. If, by contrast, part of the problem can be
addressed through endogenous regulation, then a different opportunity
emerges: multi-agent systems may be designed less as scripted workflows and
more as adaptive cognitive ecologies. This distinction matters for the
development of socially relevant applications in domains such as policy
analysis, scientific assistance, education, and other forms of persistent AI
coordination.

Our results suggest that the answer is not ``either/or'' but depends on what
properties of coordination matter. Endogenous regulation achieves comparable
argument quality to centralized routing while producing emergent structural
properties---balanced participation, homeostatic oscillation, and
stimulus-responsive activation---that centralized approaches do not produce
by design in the present experiment.

The contributions of this paper are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A theory of homeostatic agency} grounded in systemic
          materialism, allostatic regulation, and eudaimonic motivation
          research---making autonomy, drive, and quality-gated discharge
          explicit, falsifiable objects of analysis rather than design
          conventions.
    \item \textbf{An endogenous activation paradigm} in which participation
          frequency emerges from the agent's internal regulatory state rather
          than being externally imposed---a structural departure from the
          heartbeat/scheduler model.
    \item \textbf{Empirical evidence that neutral AI routers replicate the
          Michels dynamic}: external coordination concentrates turns on the
          contextually dominant faction; endogenous homeostatic regulation
          counters this tendency without explicit fairness constraints.
\end{enumerate}
