\title{LangClaw: Endogenous Homeostatic Regulation for Mitigating Context
Collapse in Multi-Agent LLM Debate Systems}

\begin{abstract}
Multi-agent systems built on Large Language Models are increasingly relevant
for domains that require sustained reasoning rather than isolated prompt
response. Their practical value, however, depends on whether coherence can be
preserved as interaction unfolds. A frequently reported problem in this
setting is \emph{context collapse}: as conversations grow, agents may lose
coherence, repeat themselves, or produce disconnected monologues. Existing
frameworks typically address this through static orchestration---fixed
turn-taking or centralized routers---but these approaches do not adapt to
the evolving state of the discourse. This motivates a broader question:
what if multi-agent coherence could be regulated endogenously rather than
imposed externally? We present LangClaw, a framework that replaces static scheduling with an
endogenous homeostatic drive. Each agent maintains an internal epistemic
deficit that grows over time and is reduced only by producing structurally
integrated arguments. A sigmoid activation function maps this deficit to a
participation probability, creating a self-regulating cycle. We measure
argument quality through a network-theoretic proxy of Integrated Information
Theory, combining betweenness centrality, cycle detection, and agent
diversity. We evaluate LangClaw against a round-robin baseline with matched
debate budgets in a zero-sum political debate with four GPT-4o-mini agents
arranged in symmetric cognitive archetypes. The proposed benchmark is
designed to test whether homeostatic regulation yields higher argument
quality per intervention than static orchestration under the same number
of debates, and whether it produces qualitatively different deficit dynamics.

\keywords{multi-agent systems \and large language models \and homeostasis \and
reinforcement learning \and context collapse}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The rapid expansion of Large Language Model (LLM) applications has renewed
interest in multi-agent systems capable of deliberating, coordinating, and
solving tasks through sustained interaction. This trend creates an important
opportunity: if multi-agent LLM systems can remain coherent over time, they
could become useful in domains such as decision support, scientific
discovery, legal and policy analysis, education, and complex digital
workflows. More broadly, they could accelerate the emergence of new software
industries centered on persistent AI coordination rather than isolated prompt
response.

At the same time, this shift exposes a pressing limitation. When interaction
extends over time, the collective process tends to degrade. Agents may lose
track of prior claims, repeat points, or produce monologues with little
structural relation to the ongoing discourse
\parencite{park2023generative}. A recurrent problem reported in this context
is what we describe here as \emph{context collapse}. Rather than treating it
as a settled property of all LLM systems, we use the term to frame a class of
observed failures that deserves systematic study. The issue is not merely
technical. If unresolved, it limits the reliability of multi-agent AI in
settings where sustained reasoning, institutional memory, and coherent
interaction are socially consequential.

The dominant response to this problem has been to increase external control.
Frameworks such as AutoGen~\parencite{wu2023autogen} and
MetaGPT~\parencite{hong2023metagpt} impose fixed turn-taking, centralized
coordinators, or predefined communication graphs to regulate when each agent
speaks. These approaches can be effective, but they also reflect a paradigm:
when coordination degrades, the solution is assumed to be more routing, more
scheduling, and more explicit supervision. This paradigm has a limitation.
When the discourse shifts, the schedule does not necessarily shift with it.
As a result, static orchestration may address the visible symptom of disorder
without necessarily explaining why the system loses coherence in the first
place.

An apparently unrelated phenomenon in biological systems offers a different
perspective: homeostasis. In living organisms, regulation does not always
depend on an external controller; it emerges from internal variables that
accumulate, trigger action, and return to equilibrium. On its own, this
observation belongs to physiology, not to multi-agent AI.

But the right question can bridge the gap. \emph{What if multi-agent
coherence could be regulated endogenously, rather than imposed externally?}
Under this question, homeostasis is no longer just a biological analogy---it
becomes an unexploited operational principle. Homeostatic regulation has
already been formalized in reinforcement learning
\parencite{keramati2014homeostatic} and extended to continuous domains
\parencite{laurencon2024ctcs}, yet it has not been applied to the problem of
LLM-based multi-agent orchestration. More specifically: \emph{can
homeostatic regulation mitigate context collapse more effectively than static
orchestration in sustained multi-agent debate?}

The importance of this question lies not only in comparing two engineering
choices, but in contrasting two paradigms of coordination. If the quality of
multi-agent reasoning depends on internal regulation rather than only on
external scheduling, then many current orchestration strategies may be
addressing the symptom rather than the cause.

To explore this question, we propose LangClaw, a framework where each agent
carries an internal \emph{epistemic deficit} that governs its participation.
The deficit grows when the agent is idle and shrinks when it produces a
high-quality contribution. A sigmoid function maps this deficit to an action
probability. The resulting system is self-regulating: agents tend to
intervene when their internal pressure rises and remain silent after a
structurally useful contribution. The mechanism is inspired by homeostatic
regulation in biological systems. To the best of our knowledge, this
perspective has not been evaluated as an orchestration principle for
LLM-based debate agents.

In prior work, we introduced AopifyJS~\parencite{gerpe2023aopifyjs}, an
agent-oriented programming framework that integrated multi-criteria
decision-making methods with an event-based communication architecture.
That work identified the need for ``more human-like abstractions for machine
argumentation'' and ``integrations with machine learning algorithms, reward
and utility functions.'' LangClaw is framed as a continuation of those open
directions. Whereas AopifyJS used deterministic decision algorithms (AHP,
TOPSIS) for agent behavior, LangClaw introduces a homeostatic drive
regulated by reinforcement learning, avoiding predefined criteria
weightings.

More specifically, this paper makes three contributions. First, it
formalizes an endogenous regulation mechanism for LLM agents through an
epistemic deficit, sigmoid activation, and quality-proportional satiation.
Second, it operationalizes argumentative quality through a graph-based
$\Phi^*$ proxy and a reproducible benchmark against static baselines. Third,
it extends the event-driven architecture explored in AopifyJS toward a
setting centered on sustained argumentative interaction. Taken together,
these contributions are intended not only to introduce a new framework, but
also to make the experimental question itself more explicit and testable.

For the sake of clarity, this paper is structured as follows: we first
review related work on multi-agent LLM systems and homeostatic reinforcement
learning; then we detail the LangClaw method, including the epistemic drive,
the argument graph, and the evaluation metric; after that, we present our
comparative benchmark against static baselines; and finally we discuss our
findings and limitations.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Multi-Agent LLM Systems}

Frameworks such as AutoGen~\parencite{wu2023autogen},
CAMEL~\parencite{li2023camel}, and MetaGPT~\parencite{hong2023metagpt}
implement multi-agent collaboration through predefined roles and
communication protocols. In practice, these frameworks rely on static
orchestration.
\textcite{huang2024masresilience} demonstrate that linear-chain orchestration
degrades $5\times$ faster than hierarchical structures under adversarial
agents, suggesting that rigid schedulers can be sensitive to interaction
stress.

\subsection{Homeostatic Reinforcement Learning}

\textcite{keramati2014homeostatic} introduced the HRRL framework, establishing the
equivalence between drive minimization and reward maximization in discrete
state spaces. \textcite{laurencon2024ctcs} extended this to continuous
time-space, formalizing the drive function
$d(\delta) = \sqrt{\varepsilon + \delta^\top\delta}$. We are not aware of
previous studies applying HRRL to LLM-based cognitive agents.

\subsection{Desire-Driven Autonomous Agents}

More recently, work on desire-driven and intrinsically motivated LLM agents
has begun to explore internal states as a basis for action selection.
\textcite{wang2025d2a} present D2A (ICLR~2025), which equips LLM agents with
a multidimensional desire vector updated through reflexive reasoning. The
main difference with our proposal is that D2A does not define a closed
homeostatic loop with an external quality signal. Its desire update depends
primarily on the LLM's self-assessment, whereas LangClaw introduces a formal
sigmoid threshold, quality-proportional satiation, and an external signal
($\Delta\varphi^*$) derived from the evolving argument graph.

\subsection{Agent-Oriented Programming}

\textcite{gerpe2023aopifyjs} proposed AopifyJS as a framework combining AOSE
high-level abstractions with multi-criteria decision-making methods. That
work showed that agent entities with self-contained memory and event-based
communication provide a suitable architecture for modeling human-machine
interaction. LangClaw preserves the event-driven design pattern---agents
communicate through asynchronous event queues---but replaces deterministic
MCDC algorithms with a homeostatic regulation loop and LLM-based reasoning.

\subsection{Other Relevant Work}

The Sotopia framework~\parencite{zhou2024sotopia} evaluates LLM agents in
social interactions. We adopt its zero-sum structure but focus on
argumentative coherence. Defeasible Logic Programming
(DeLP)~\parencite{garcia2004defeasible} provides formal semantics for
argument graphs. Integrated Information Theory
(IIT)~\parencite{tononi2016integrated} quantifies informational integration;
we use a network-theoretic proxy. In-context
learning~\parencite{brown2020language} enables LLMs to learn from prompt
examples; we extend this to reinforcement learning.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{The Epistemic Drive}

A key design decision is how to represent the internal state that governs
agent participation. Alternative approaches include token-counting heuristics
(participate after $n$ tokens of silence), attention-based triggers
(participate when attention to recent context drops below a threshold), or
purely random activation. We chose a scalar epistemic deficit for three
reasons. First, it is interpretable: the deficit has a clear meaning (how
much the agent ``needs'' to contribute). Second, it is dynamically coupled
to output quality through the satiation mechanism, creating a closed
feedback loop. Third, it is compatible with the HRRL framework
\parencite{keramati2014homeostatic}, which provides a theoretical grounding
for the equivalence between drive minimization and reward maximization.

Each agent $i$ maintains an internal variable
$\delta_i \in [\varepsilon, \infty)$, the epistemic deficit, with resting
baseline $\varepsilon = 0.1$. Three mechanisms govern it.

\subsubsection{Decay.}
At each tick $t$, the deficit grows:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_i^{(t+1)} = \delta_i^{(t)} + \lambda
    \label{eq:decay}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 0.05$. This models the pressure to contribute.

\subsubsection{Sigmoid Activation.}
The probability that agent $i$ acts at tick $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
    p_i^{(t)} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k(\delta_i^{(t)} - \theta)}}
    \label{eq:sigmoid}
\end{equation}
where $k = 10$ and $\theta = 0.7$. Below $\theta$ the agent rests; above it,
the agent is compelled to participate.

\subsubsection{Satiation.}
After producing an argument with informational value $\Delta\varphi^*$:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_i^{\text{new}} = \max\!\bigl(\varepsilon,\;
        \delta_i - \alpha \cdot \Delta\varphi^*\bigr)
    \label{eq:satiation}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 2.0$. If $\Delta\varphi^* = 0$ (an isolated monologue), the
deficit does not decrease. This is the key mechanism against context
collapse: only structurally connected contributions provide relief.

\subsection{Argument Graph and $\Phi^*$ Proxy}

A central design choice is how to measure the quality of each argumentative
intervention. Standard NLP metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, or BERTScore
evaluate surface-level textual similarity, but they do not capture
\emph{structural integration}---whether an argument connects meaningfully to
the existing discourse. Perplexity-based metrics assess fluency but not
logical relevance. What we need is a measure that is sensitive to the
\emph{position} of an argument within the evolving debate network: does it
engage with central claims? Does it create dialectical cycles? Does it
involve multiple perspectives?

For this reason, we use a network-theoretic proxy inspired by Integrated
Information Theory (IIT)~\parencite{tononi2016integrated}. IIT proposes that
informational integration---the degree to which a system is more than the sum
of its parts---can be quantified through the structure of its causal
connections. Computing the full $\Phi$ measure is intractable for arbitrary
graphs, so we approximate it through three graph-theoretic features that
capture complementary aspects of informational integration.

The discourse is represented as a directed graph $G = (V, E)$. Nodes are
claims; edges are logical attacks (undercut or rebuttal). When agent $i$
adds node $v$ attacking target $u$, the quality is:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta\varphi^* = 0.35 \cdot C_B(u) + 0.35 \cdot
        \mathbb{1}_{\text{cycle}}(v, u) + 0.30 \cdot D(u)
    \label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}

where $C_B(u)$ is the betweenness centrality of the target,
$\mathbb{1}_{\text{cycle}}$ indicates whether a refutation cycle was created,
and $D(u)$ is the ratio of distinct agents neighboring the target. Isolated
nodes get $\Delta\varphi^* = 0$.

Each component was chosen for a specific reason. Betweenness centrality
$C_B(u)$ captures whether the target is a structurally important node in the
discourse---attacking a peripheral claim contributes less than engaging with
a claim that connects multiple lines of argumentation. The cycle indicator
$\mathbb{1}_{\text{cycle}}$ rewards the creation of dialectical loops
(A attacks B, B attacks C, C attacks A), which are a hallmark of genuine
deliberation as opposed to parallel monologues. Agent diversity $D(u)$
measures whether the target has been engaged by multiple agents, favoring
arguments that enter contested territory rather than isolated exchanges
between two agents. Given that this is a heuristic proxy rather than a
theoretically grounded measure, the weights ($0.35$, $0.35$, $0.30$) were
set to give roughly equal importance to all three features. Their
optimization is left for future work.

\subsection{Action Space}

Beyond DEBATE/PASS, agents select from four actions via a utility selector:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DEBATE}: Produce an argument (requires LLM call).
    \item \textbf{SEARCH}: Acquire a domain fact via Tavily web search or a
          static fallback pool.
    \item \textbf{READ}: Absorb a recent observation from working memory.
    \item \textbf{PASS}: Rest.
\end{itemize}

The utility function for each action depends on the agent's deficit, memory
density, and graph state. The implementation is straightforward:

\begin{lstlisting}
scores = selector.scores(drive, memory, graph_nodes)
action = max(scores, key=scores.get)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Three-Layer Memory}

Each agent maintains: (1)~episodic memory---past experiences sorted by
$|\Delta\varphi^*|$ so both successes and failures stay visible;
(2)~semantic memory---facts accumulated through SEARCH actions;
(3)~working memory---a sliding window of recently observed arguments.
The top experiences and facts are injected into the LLM prompt, enabling
in-context reinforcement learning without parameter updates.

\subsection{Event-Driven Architecture}

Following the event-based communication pattern established in
AopifyJS~\parencite{gerpe2023aopifyjs}, agents communicate through
asynchronous event queues. Three event types drive the simulation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{TickElapsedEvent}: Triggers drive decay in all agents.
    \item \texttt{NewArgumentEvent}: Broadcasts a new argument to all peers.
    \item \texttt{SimulationEndEvent}: Signals shutdown.
\end{itemize}

Each agent runs as an independent coroutine that processes events from its
queue and publishes results to a shared output queue. The simulation
environment reads from this queue to update the graph and broadcast events.
No agent has direct access to another agent's state.

\subsection{Agent Roles: Symmetric Design}

A critical design decision is role symmetry. In early iterations, we used
asymmetric roles (e.g., ``deflector'' vs.\ ``logical analyst''), but this
confounded the comparison between orchestration modes. We redesigned the
roles to mirror Analytical and Strategic archetypes across factions:

\begin{table}
\caption{Agent roles with symmetric cognitive archetypes.}\label{tab:roles}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Agent} & \textbf{Faction} & \textbf{Archetype} &
    \textbf{Objective} \\
\hline
GOV-1 & Gov. & Analytical & Defend with data, find factual
    inconsistencies in opposition \\
GOV-2 & Gov. & Strategic & Reframe criticism, challenge premises
    and fallacies \\
OPP-1 & Opp. & Analytical & Attack with data, find factual
    inconsistencies in government \\
OPP-2 & Opp. & Strategic & Reframe defenses, challenge premises
    and fallacies \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

This ensures that performance differences reflect the orchestration
mechanism, not role-inherent advantages.

\subsection{Orchestration Modes}

We compare two modes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HRRL}: Agents run as async coroutines regulated by their
          drive. No external scheduler. Agents participate only when their
          deficit crosses the activation threshold.
    \item \textbf{Round-Robin}: Every agent speaks every tick. Static
          baseline representing the dominant external-control paradigm.
\end{itemize}

To ensure a fair comparison, the benchmark matches debate budgets: HRRL
runs for $T$ ticks and produces $N$ debates organically; Round-Robin then
runs for $\lceil N / n_{\text{agents}} \rceil$ ticks, so that both modes
consume approximately the same number of LLM interventions. This design
controls for the volume confound that would arise from Round-Robin
producing $n_{\text{agents}} \times T$ debates while HRRL produces fewer.

\subsection{Evaluation: $\tau$-bench}

Evaluating multi-agent debate requires a metric that captures whether agents
are actually engaging with each other's arguments, as opposed to producing
independent monologues that happen to coexist in the same transcript.
Standard dialogue evaluation metrics---such as coherence scores based on
embedding similarity or human preference rankings---are either too expensive
for automated benchmarking or insufficiently sensitive to structural
disconnection. What we need is a metric that directly measures whether each
argument \emph{targets} an existing claim in the discourse graph.

We therefore define $\tau$-bench as the fraction of DEBATE turns that are
logically consistent---i.e., the argument connects to an existing node via a
valid attack:
\begin{equation}
    \tau = \frac{|\{a \in A_{\text{debate}} :
        \text{target}(a) \in V\}|}{|A_{\text{debate}}|}
    \label{eq:tau}
\end{equation}

A $\tau$-bench of 1.0 means every argument engaged with the existing
discourse. A $\tau$-bench near 0 means agents are monologuing. We chose this
metric because it is binary, unambiguous, and computable directly from the
graph structure without requiring human annotation or model-based scoring.
Its limitation is that it does not distinguish between a superficially
connected argument and a deeply relevant one; for that purpose, the
$\Delta\varphi^*$ proxy provides a complementary measure of per-argument
quality.

\subsection{Research Question and Experimental Expectations}

The central research question of the experiment is whether endogenous
homeostatic regulation can preserve argumentative coherence better than
static orchestration under the same task, model, and seed conditions.

Before running the benchmark, we expect two main patterns. First, with
debate budgets matched, HRRL should achieve higher average $\Delta\varphi^*$
than Round-Robin, because its selective participation should produce more
structurally integrated arguments per intervention. Second, we expect HRRL
deficits to oscillate around the activation threshold $\theta$, exhibiting
a self-regulating cycle, whereas Round-Robin deficits should behave
differently since participation is forced regardless of internal state.
With symmetric roles across factions, we expect that observed differences
between agents are more plausibly attributable to the orchestration regime
than to role asymmetry.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Benchmark Design}

We run the same simulation under HRRL and Round-Robin using identical
configuration (GPT-4o-mini, $T = 50$ for HRRL). Crucially, debate budgets
are matched: HRRL runs for $T$ ticks and produces $N$ debates organically;
Round-Robin then runs for $\lceil N/4 \rceil$ ticks so both modes consume
approximately the same number of interventions. This controls for the
volume confound. We repeat the experiment with three seeds
($s \in \{42, 123, 256\}$) and report mean values. This design allows us
to ask the data a constrained question: given the same debate budget, which
orchestration regime produces higher-quality argumentation?

\subsection{Phase Analysis (HRRL)}

The HRRL simulation exhibits three phases:

\textbf{Phase 1: Accumulation.} All agents pass. Deficits grow linearly
toward $\theta$. No arguments are produced.

\textbf{Phase 2: Onset.} Deficits cross $\theta = 0.7$. The first debates
appear. The graph begins to form.

\textbf{Phase 3: Equilibrium.} Deficits oscillate around $\theta$. Acting
reduces the deficit via satiation, which reduces the probability of acting,
which lets the deficit grow again. This is the homeostatic cycle.

This phase structure is consistent with the expected behavior of a closed
homeostatic loop: accumulation, activation, and regulation are not imposed
by an external controller, but emerge from the interaction between deficit,
threshold, and satiation.

The benchmark data confirms the three-phase structure across all seeds. In
HRRL, agents pass during ticks 1--4, begin debating around tick 5, and then
oscillate. In contrast, Round-Robin forces all agents to speak every tick;
because satiation reduces deficits after each high-quality argument, agents
end at heterogeneous deficit levels ($0.29$--$0.57$) depending on the
quality of their individual contributions, even though all were forced to
participate equally. This suggests that context preservation in multi-agent
debate depends not only on who speaks, but on how the system regulates the
conditions under which speaking becomes likely.

\subsection{Agent-Level Analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:agents} presents per-agent metrics averaged across seeds.
Under HRRL, participation is distributed across agents (7--8.3 debates per
agent on average), with variation driven by the homeostatic mechanism: agents
that produce higher-quality arguments receive more satiation and may
participate slightly less or more depending on the deficit-threshold
interplay. Final deficits cluster tightly around the activation threshold
($\delta \approx 0.64$--$0.68$), consistent with the oscillatory equilibrium
predicted by the model.

Under Round-Robin, all agents produce the same number of debates (one per
tick), but final deficits diverge more widely ($0.29$--$0.57$). This
reflects that some agents produced higher-quality arguments and received
more satiation even though all were forced to participate equally.
Importantly, Round-Robin does not use this deficit information for
scheduling---agents speak regardless of their internal state.

A noteworthy observation is that GOV-1, the Analytical government agent,
shows the largest quality gap between modes: $\Delta\varphi^* = 0.1403$
under HRRL vs.\ $0.0840$ under Round-Robin. This could be explained by the
fact that under HRRL, GOV-1 only intervenes when its deficit warrants
participation, leading to more targeted attacks on high-centrality nodes.
Under Round-Robin, forced participation dilutes argument quality. The
symmetric design makes this comparison possible: because both factions share
each archetype, observed differences are attributable to the orchestration
mode rather than to factional advantage.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Results}

With debate budgets matched, the comparison between HRRL and Round-Robin
becomes more informative than previous uncontrolled comparisons. Two main
findings emerge.

First, $\tau$-bench is comparable between modes ($0.9585$ vs.\ $0.9696$),
suggesting that the ability to produce logically connected arguments is
similar when both modes operate under the same debate volume. The small
advantage for Round-Robin is within the seed-to-seed variability observed
for HRRL (std = 0.015). This metric alone does not differentiate the modes.

Second, HRRL achieves consistently higher per-argument quality
($\Delta\varphi^* = 0.1429$ vs.\ $0.1275$, a 12\% improvement). This is
consistent with the hypothesis that internally regulated participation
produces more structurally integrated arguments: agents intervene only when
their deficit crosses the activation threshold, leading to more deliberate
target selection. The deficit-sigmoid loop creates an endogenous governor
where agents that have contributed meaningfully become temporarily
quiescent, whereas agents that have remained silent accumulate pressure to
speak.

The most informative observation concerns deficit dynamics. Under HRRL,
final deficits cluster tightly around the activation threshold
($0.64$--$0.68$), consistent with oscillatory self-regulation. Under
Round-Robin, deficits diverge more widely ($0.29$--$0.57$) depending on
individual argument quality, but this information is not used for scheduling.
This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that endogenous regulation
produces a qualitatively different dynamic than static scheduling.

From a broader perspective, the contrast is not simply between two
implementation choices. It is a contrast between two ways of understanding
orchestration. One treats coordination as an externally scripted process.
The other treats it as a property that can emerge from local regulation.
The present results, based on three seeded runs with matched budgets,
suggest that the homeostatic lens produces observable, interpretable
differences in system behavior---particularly in per-argument quality and
deficit dynamics---that aggregate metrics like $\tau$-bench alone do not
fully capture.

\subsection{From AopifyJS to LangClaw}

This work extends the agent-based architecture introduced in
AopifyJS~\parencite{gerpe2023aopifyjs} along the directions identified as
future work in that paper. Three specific advances correspond to those open
problems:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Human-like abstractions}: The epistemic drive provides
          agents with an internal motivational state analogous to biological
          homeostasis, replacing the static decision criteria of MCDC methods.
    \item \textbf{Machine argumentation}: The argument graph with undercut
          and rebuttal relations implements formal argumentation semantics,
          moving beyond the request-response pattern of AopifyJS.
    \item \textbf{Reward and utility functions}: The $\Phi^*$ proxy and the
          utility selector provide the reinforcement learning integration that
          AopifyJS identified as a next step.
\end{enumerate}

The event-driven architecture is preserved: agents communicate through
asynchronous queues, maintain self-contained state, and store interaction
history---the same principles that AopifyJS established for
interpretability and scalability.

\subsection{Limitations}

We identify five limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Statistical power}: A single seeded run is insufficient for
          rigorous claims. Multiple seeds and ablation studies are needed.
    \item \textbf{$\Phi^*$ is a proxy}: The weights ($0.35, 0.35, 0.30$)
          were set heuristically and not optimized.
    \item \textbf{LLM dependence}: Results will vary with different models,
          temperatures, and prompt formulations.
    \item \textbf{Scale}: Four agents and 30--50 ticks is small. Behavior
          with more agents or longer horizons is untested.
    \item \textbf{Search coverage}: Web search depends on API availability
          and query quality.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Further Research}

Several directions follow from this work. First, multi-run statistical
analysis with confidence intervals would strengthen the comparative claims.
Second, hyperparameter sensitivity studies---particularly for $\theta$,
$\alpha$, and $\lambda$---would characterize the robustness of the
homeostatic equilibrium. Third, scaling to larger agent populations and
longer horizons would test whether the self-regulation property holds.
Finally, replacing the heuristic $\Phi^*$ proxy with a learned quality
function could improve the fidelity of the homeostatic signal.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper examined a concrete question: whether context collapse in
multi-agent LLM debate is better approached through external scheduling or
through endogenous regulation. We introduced LangClaw as an experimental
framework for studying that question under controlled conditions, comparing a
homeostatic mechanism against a round-robin baseline with matched debate
budgets, while reducing role-related confounds through symmetric agent
design.

The relevance of this question extends beyond the present prototype. If
coherence in multi-agent interaction depends mainly on external control, then
future systems will likely continue to evolve toward more sophisticated
routing and supervision layers. If, by contrast, part of the problem can be
addressed through endogenous regulation, then a different opportunity
emerges: multi-agent systems may be designed less as scripted workflows and
more as adaptive cognitive ecologies. This distinction matters for the
development of socially relevant applications in domains such as policy
analysis, scientific assistance, education, and other forms of persistent AI
coordination.

Our expectation throughout the paper has been that homeostatic control should
better preserve coherence because it links participation to the evolving
state of the discourse rather than to a fixed schedule. In that sense, the
value of LangClaw lies not only in the mechanism itself, but in providing a
testable way to ask whether a concept drawn from a different paradigm can
illuminate a current limitation in LLM-based multi-agent systems.

The major contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A formal specification of homeostatic regulation (HRRL) for
          multi-agent LLM systems, including the epistemic drive model,
          sigmoid activation, and quality-proportional satiation.
    \item An implementable $\Phi^*$ proxy that measures argument quality
          through network-theoretic features.
    \item A comparative benchmark framework that evaluates HRRL against
          static baselines under identical conditions.
    \item Symmetric agent role design that eliminates confounding role bias.
    \item An evolution of the AopifyJS event-driven architecture toward
          homeostatic agent autonomy.
\end{itemize}
